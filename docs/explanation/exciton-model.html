Two-Component Exciton Model

Core idea: A nuclear reaction proceeds through a hierarchy of increasingly complex configurations, from the initial interaction to statistical equilibrium. The exciton model describes this evolution by tracking the number of excited particles and holes above the Fermi surface, grouped by isospin (proton vs. neutron).

1. Particle–Hole States

An exciton state is defined by four integers:

\[ |p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu\rangle \]

where \(p_\pi\) and \(h_\pi\) are the number of proton particles and holes, and \(p_\nu\) and \(h_\nu\) are the number of neutron particles and holes. The total exciton number is:

\[ n = p_\pi + h_\pi + p_\nu + h_\nu \]

and the total particle/hole numbers are:

\[ p = p_\pi + p_\nu, \qquad h = h_\pi + h_\nu \]

For the n + 40Ar system at 20 MeV incident energy, the compound nucleus 41Ar has excitation energy Ecomp = 25.60156 MeV. The code enumerates all states with \(k = (p+h)/2\) from 0 to 5 (84 states for lambda, 300 for omega including spin distributions).

2. The Master Equation

The time evolution of the exciton population is governed by:

\[ \frac{dP(n,t)}{dt} = \lambda^+(n-2) P(n-2,t) + \lambda^-(n+2) P(n+2,t) - [\lambda^+(n) + \lambda^-(n) + \lambda^0_{\pi\nu}(n) + \lambda^0_{\nu\pi}(n)] P(n,t) \]

where:

  • \(\lambda^+(n)\) — internal transition rate (pair creation): \(n \to n+2\)
  • \(\lambda^-(n)\) — internal transition rate (pair annihilation): \(n \to n-2\)
  • \(\lambda^0(n)\) — internal conversion rate: \(n \to n\) (proton ↔ neutron, no change in n)

This code computes the forward rates \(\lambda^+\) and \(\lambda^0\) for each state. The pair-annihilation rate \(\lambda^-\) is related to \(\lambda^+\) through detailed balance but is not computed here — the focus is on the initial pre-equilibrium emission from the compound system.

3. Two-Component Formalism

The extension from one-component (Griffin 1966) to two-component (Kalbach 1986) is essential because:

  • Proton and neutron single-particle state densities differ: \(g_\pi = Z/15 \ne g_\nu = N/15\)
  • The residual interaction distinguishes same-type (ππ, νν) and cross-type (πν, νπ) collisions
  • Isospin mixing is naturally handled through conversion channels (π↔ν)
