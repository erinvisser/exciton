Particle-Hole State Densities

Understanding the combinatorial foundation of the exciton model.

What Is a State Density?

The particle-hole state density \(\omega(p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu, E_x)\) counts the number of ways to distribute \(n = p_\pi + h_\pi + p_\nu + h_\nu\) excitons among the single-particle states at excitation energy \(E_x\). It is the density of accessible nuclear configurations at a given energy — the phase space available to the pre-equilibrium reaction.

The Combinatorial Formula

The state density (Eq. 13.4) has three factors:

\[ \omega(p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu, E_x) = \underbrace{\frac{g_p^{n_\pi}\, g_n^{n_\nu}} {p_\pi!\, h_\pi!\, p_\nu!\, h_\nu!\, (n-1)!}}_{\text{Combinatorial factor}} \times \underbrace{(E_{\text{avail}})^{n-1}}_{\text{Energy factor}} \times \underbrace{f_{\text{FW}}(p, h, U, V)}_{\text{Finite well correction}} \]

1. The Combinatorial Factor

This factor counts the number of distinct exciton configurations. For each particle type (protons and neutrons), the number of ways to choose \(p\) particles among \(g\) single-particle states and \(h\) holes among the same \(g\) states, with the exclusion that particles and holes cannot occupy the same state, is:

\[ \frac{g^p}{p!} \cdot \frac{g^h}{h!} \]

The factor \(1/(n-1)!\) accounts for the indistinguishability of the \(n\) excitons in the energy-sharing configuration. For the two-component model, proton and neutron single-particle densities are separate: \(g_p = Z/15\) and \(g_n = N/15\).

2. The Energy Factor

After accounting for the minimum energy required by the Pauli principle, the remaining energy \(E_{\text{avail}}\) is shared among \(n\) excitons. The density of states in \(n-1\) dimensional phase space scales as \(E^{n-1}\).

The available energy is:

\[ E_{\text{avail}} = U - A(p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu) \]

where \(U = E_x - f_u\) (excitation energy minus pairing correction) and \(A\) is the Pauli blocking correction.

3. The Finite Well Correction

The finite well correction accounts for the fact that the nuclear potential is not infinitely deep. When the excitation energy approaches the well depth \(V\), the number of accessible hole states decreases because holes cannot be created beyond the bottom of the well:

\[ f_{\text{FW}}(p, h, U, V) = 1 + \sum_{i=1}^{h} (-1)^i \binom{h}{i} \left(\frac{U - iV}{U}\right)^{n-1} \Theta(U - iV) \]

For well depths much larger than the excitation energy (\(V \gg U\)), this correction is close to 1. For \(U > V\), the correction alters the state density as hole states begin to be blocked.

The Pauli Correction

The Pauli correction \(A(p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu)\) accounts for the minimum energy required to create a given exciton configuration while respecting the Pauli exclusion principle:

\[ A = \frac{\max(p_\pi, h_\pi)^2}{g_p} + \frac{\max(p_\nu, h_\nu)^2}{g_n} - \frac{p_\pi^2 + h_\pi^2 + p_\pi + h_\pi}{4 g_p} - \frac{p_\nu^2 + h_\nu^2 + p_\nu + h_\nu}{4 g_n} \]

The first two terms set the minimum energy based on the larger of the particle/hole numbers for each type. The subtracted terms account for the average lowering due to the indistinguishability of particles and holes of the same type.

Pairing Effects

The pairing correction accounts for the odd-even staggering of nuclear masses. The pairing energy is:

\[ \Delta(A, \chi) = \chi \cdot \frac{12}{\sqrt{A}},\qquad \chi = \begin{cases}0 & \text{odd-odd}\\1 & \text{odd-A}\\2 & \text{even-even}\end{cases} \]

The empirical function \(f_u(E_x/\Delta, n/n_{\text{crit}})\) suppresses the state density at low excitation energies and transitions to unpaired behavior at higher energies:

\[ f_u(E_x/\Delta, n/n_{\text{crit}}) = \begin{cases} \Delta & E_x/\Delta < \text{threshold} \\[4pt] \Delta - \Delta\Bigl[0.996 - 1.76\,(n/n_{\text{crit}})^{1.6}\,(E_x/\Delta)^{-0.68}\Bigr]^2 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Spin Distribution

The spin distribution \(R_n(J)\) gives the fraction of states with total angular momentum \(J\) at exciton number \(n\):

\[ R_n(J) = \frac{2J+1}{\sqrt{\pi}\, k^{3/2}\, \sigma^3} \exp\!\left[-\frac{(J+\tfrac12)^2}{k\,\sigma^2}\right], \quad \sigma^2 = 0.24\, n\, A^{2/3} \]

The spin cutoff parameter \(\sigma^2\) depends on the mass number \(A\) and exciton number \(n\). The parameter \(k\) distinguishes between the TALYS-compatible formula (\(k=2\)) and the original formulation (\(k=n\)).

Behavior at Low Excitation Energy

For a given exciton configuration, the state density is zero when:

  • The excitation energy is below the Pauli threshold: \(E_x < \max(p_\pi, h_\pi)^2/g_p + \max(p_\nu, h_\nu)^2/g_n\)
  • The available energy after the pairing correction is negative
  • The total exciton number \(n < 1\)

This is why for Ar-40, the 2p2h configurations have zero density at Ex < 5–6 MeV.

Critical Exciton Number

The critical exciton number \(n_{\text{crit}}\) marks the transition from pre-equilibrium to equilibrium behavior. It is the exciton number at which the system can be considered fully equilibrated:

\[ n_{\text{crit}} = 2 g T_{\text{crit}} \ln 2, \qquad T_{\text{crit}} = \frac{2}{3.5} \sqrt{\frac{\Delta}{0.25 g}} \]

For Ar-40 (odd-A, \(\chi=1\)), the pairing gap is \(\Delta \approx 1.87\) MeV, the single-particle density \(g = g_p + g_n \approx 2.73\) MeV-1, and \(n_{\text{crit}} \approx 21\). This means the system is expected to equilibrate at around 21 excitons.
