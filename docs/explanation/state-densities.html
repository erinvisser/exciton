Particle–Hole State Densities

Role: The particle–hole state density \(\omega(p,h,E)\) counts the number of ways to distribute excitation energy E among n excitons. It is the statistical weight that enters every transition rate formula as a density-of-final-states factor.

1. Two-Component Formula

The two-component Williams formula (Eq. 13.4) for the state density of configuration \(|p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu\rangle\) at excitation energy Ex is:

\[ \omega(p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu, E_x) = \frac{g_\pi^{p_\pi + h_\pi} \, g_\nu^{p_\nu + h_\nu}} {p_\pi! \, h_\pi! \, p_\nu! \, h_\nu! \, (n-1)!} \; U^{n-1} \; f_{\text{Pauli}}(p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu) \; f_{\text{well}}(p, h, U, V) \; f_{\text{pairing}}(E_x, n, n_{\text{crit}}) \; f_{\text{parity}} \]

The implementation is in src/state_density.cpp:17:

double particleHoleStateDensity(
  int p_pi, int h_pi, int p_nu, int h_nu,
  double E_x, int Z, int N, int chi,
  double V, double n_crit,
  bool applyPairing = true,
  double parityFactor = 1.0);

2. The Factorial Denominator

The denominator \(p_\pi! \, h_\pi! \, p_\nu! \, h_\nu! \, (n-1)!\) accounts for the indistinguishability of particles and holes within each isospin group, and for the fact that the first exciton is "pinned" (the incident nucleon). The factorial is computed iteratively in factorial().

3. Single-Particle Densities

Ericson's formula gives the single-particle state density at the Fermi surface:

\[ g_\pi = \frac{Z}{15}, \qquad g_\nu = \frac{N}{15} \]

These values are in MeV-1. For 41Ar (compound): gπ = 1.2, gν = 1.5333. The product \(g_\pi^{p_\pi + h_\pi} g_\nu^{p_\nu + h_\nu}\) multiplies the single-particle densities for each exciton in the configuration.

4. Pauli Correction

The Pauli correction (Eq. 13.9) reduces the available phase space by accounting for the fact that particles and holes must occupy distinct single-particle states:

\[ P(p_\pi, h_\pi, p_\nu, h_\nu) = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{i,j} \frac{a_{ij}}{g_{ij}} \]

The full expression involves four pair terms (ππ, νν, πν, νπ) weighted by their respective single-particle densities. The correction is implemented in pauliCorrection() in src/preeq_common.cpp.

5. Finite Well Correction

The finite well depth correction (Eq. 13.16) accounts for the fact that the nuclear potential well is not infinitely deep — states near the continuum have reduced density:

\[ f_{\text{well}}(p, h, U, V) = 1 - \left(\frac{U}{U+V}\right)^{p+h} \]

where V is the Fermi well depth (default 38 MeV). For deep configurations (large n) or high excitation (U ∼ V), this correction becomes significant.

6. Pairing Correction

Pairing is handled through the back-shifted pairing model (Eqs. 13.10–13.13):

  • Pairing energy: \(\Delta = \chi \cdot 12 / \sqrt{A}\) (χ = +1 for even–even, −1 for even–odd)
  • Critical exciton number: \(n_{\text{crit}} = 2 \sqrt{g_\pi g_\nu / (g_\pi + g_\nu)} \, \Delta\)
  • For n ≥ ncrit: Pph = 1 (pairing no longer affects the state density)
  • For n < ncrit: Pph = exp[−(ncrit−n)/(ncrit−1) · Δ/U]

The available energy after pairing is U = Ex · Pph.

7. State Density in the Rate Integrand

The state density appears three times in the numerical rate integrand:

  • Initial state ωi(U) — appears in the denominator (normalization)
  • Transition state ωt(e) — density of the intermediate (created) pair at energy e
  • Residual state ωr(U−e) — density of the residual (removed) configuration at energy U−e

For conversion, three state densities appear: ωc(e) (created), ωa(e) (annihilated), ωr(U−e) (residual).

8. Spin Distribution

The spin-dependence of the state density is factorized:

\[ \omega(p,h,E,J) = \omega(p,h,E) \cdot R_n(J) \]

where Rn(J) is the spin distribution function:

\[ R_n(J) = \frac{2J+1}{2\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_n^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(J+\frac12)^2}{2\sigma_n^2}\right), \quad \sigma_n^2 = 0.28 \, n \, A^{2/3} \]

The spin distribution is validated against TALYS (0.0095% max error for J=0..8, n=1..12).
