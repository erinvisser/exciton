Optical Model Potential in the Exciton Model

/workspace/exciton/docs/omp_implementation.html — companion to transition_rates.cpp, KoningDelarocheOpticalModel.{hh,cc}, and main_lambda.cpp

Purpose: Replace the constant matrix-element approximation (M2) for nucleon–nucleon collisions inside the nucleus with a collision probability derived from the imaginary part of the nucleon–nucleus optical potential. This implements TALYS preeqmode=3.

1. Overview of the Data Flow

2. Why an Optical Model?

Physics: In the pre-equilibrium exciton model, internal transitions (creation of new particle–hole pairs) are driven by two-body residual interactions. The standard approach uses a constant matrix element M2 (Kalbach–Cline parametrization, Eq. 13.24–13.26). However, this neglects the energy and isospin dependence of the effective N–N interaction inside the nuclear medium.

The Koning–Delaroche (KD) optical model potential [1] gives the complex nucleon–nucleus interaction:

\[ U(\mathbf{r}) = V(\mathbf{r}) + i\,W(\mathbf{r}) + V_{so}(\mathbf{r})\,(\mathbf{l}\cdot\mathbf{s}) + i\,W_{so}(\mathbf{r})\,(\mathbf{l}\cdot\mathbf{s}) + V_C(\mathbf{r}) \]

The imaginary volume term \(W(r)\) describes absorption out of the elastic channel into more complex configurations — exactly the physics needed for pre-equilibrium internal transitions. We replace the constant M² with a collision probability derived from \(W(r)\).

3. The Wompfac — Bridging M² and the OMP

TALYS uses a Wompfac (weight factor) to scale the optical model strength to match the M² normalization. This factor preserves the same relative channel strengths (same-type vs. cross-type vs. conversion) while replacing the absolute scale with an OMP-driven collision rate.

The three wompfac values, computed in transition_rates.cpp:132-140 (pair creation) and transition_rates.cpp:444-453 (conversion), follow the TALYS matrix.f90 formulas:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{R}_{\pi\nu} &= \frac{R_{\pi\nu}}{R_{\pi\pi}} \\[4pt] \text{wompfac}_{\text{same}} &= \frac{M^2_c \cdot 0.55}{1 + 2\,\text{R}_{\pi\nu}} \\[4pt] \text{wompfac}_{\text{cross}} &= \frac{M^2_c \cdot 0.55 \cdot 2\,\text{R}_{\pi\nu}}{1 + 2\,\text{R}_{\pi\nu}} \\[4pt] \text{wompfac}_{\text{conv}} &= \frac{\text{wompfac}_{\text{same}} + \text{wompfac}_{\text{cross}}}{2} \end{aligned} \]
Wompfac Role Code location
wompfacsame Proton→proton or neutron→neutron pair creation transition_rates.cpp:138
wompfaccross Proton→neutron or neutron→proton pair creation transition_rates.cpp:139
wompfacconv Proton↔neutron conversion transition_rates.cpp:453
Why 0.55? The constant \(C_{\text{OMP}} = 0.55\) comes from TALYS's matrix.f90. It normalizes the relative M² strengths such that the absolute scale is set by the OMP imaginary potential. The M² constant M2c is still tunable; at M2c = 1.0, Rπν = 1.0, the default wompfac values are 0.1833, 0.3667, and 0.275.

4. Compound Nucleus and Separation Energies

The optical model collision involves the compound nucleus formed by the projectile (charge Zproj, mass Ap) and the target (Z, N). The kinetic energy of the emitted nucleon is the integration variable e (the energy of the created pair) minus its separation energy:

\[ \begin{aligned} Z_{\text{comp}} &= Z + Z_{\text{proj}} \\ N_{\text{comp}} &= N + (A_p - Z_{\text{proj}}) \\ A_{\text{comp}} &= Z_{\text{comp}} + N_{\text{comp}} \\ e_{\text{kin}} &= e - S_{p/n}(Z_{\text{comp}}, N_{\text{comp}}) \end{aligned} \]

4.1 MARLEY MassTable Separation Energies

Separation energies are obtained from the MARLEY MassTable singleton (transition_rates.cpp:189-190):

\[ \begin{aligned} S_p &= \text{MassTable::get\_fragment\_separation\_energy}(Z_{\text{comp}}, A_{\text{comp}}, \text{2212}) \\ S_n &= \text{MassTable::get\_fragment\_separation\_energy}(Z_{\text{comp}}, A_{\text{comp}}, \text{2112}) \end{aligned} \]

The MassTable uses experimental atomic masses from the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME) where available, falling back to the liquid-drop model of Koning et al. (2008) [2] for unmeasured nuclides. The MassTable data file mass_table.js is located at $MARLEY/data/mass_table.js. At runtime, the MARLEY environment variable must point to the marley-private directory so the FileManager can find it.

5. The Koning–Delaroche Optical Model

The KD model [1] is a global phenomenological optical potential fitted to elastic scattering data across the nuclear chart. For the exciton model, we need only the central imaginary potential (volume and surface absorption), not the full scattering solution.

5.1 Constructor — Parameter Initialization

KoningDelarocheOpticalModel.cc:194-255

The constructor takes the target (compound) nucleus (Z, A) and computes all geometry and energy-dependent parameters of the KD model. The key parameters for the imaginary potential are:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{W}_1^n &= 12.195 + 0.0167 A \\ \text{W}_2^n &= 73.55 + 0.0795 A \\ \text{D}_1^n &= 16.0 - 16.0 (N-Z)/A \\ \text{D}_2^n &= 0.0180 + \frac{0.003802}{1 + e^{(A-156)/8}} \\ \text{D}_3^n &= 11.5 \\ \text{E}_f^n &= -11.2814 + 0.02646 A \end{aligned} \]

Proton analogs differ in the symmetry-term sign:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{W}_1^p &= 14.667 + 0.009629 A \\ \text{D}_1^p &= 16.0 + 16.0 (N-Z)/A \\ \text{E}_f^p &= -8.4075 + 0.01378 A \end{aligned} \]

The geometry parameters (radii and diffusenesses) for the volume imaginary term:

\[ \begin{aligned} R_v^n &= 1.3039 A^{1/3} - 0.4054 \quad [\text{fm}] \\ a_v^n &= 0.6778 - 1.487\times10^{-4} A \quad [\text{fm}] \\ R_d^n &= 1.3424 A^{1/3} - 0.01585 A^{2/3} \quad [\text{fm}] \\ a_d^n &= 0.5446 - 1.656\times10^{-4} A \quad [\text{fm}] \end{aligned} \]

5.2 Energy-Dependent Potentials

KoningDelarocheOpticalModel.cc:79-192, function calculate_om_parameters()

At a given fragment kinetic energy E (with energy per nucleon \(E_{\text{eff}} = E/a\)), the imaginary potentials are:

\[ \begin{aligned} W_v(E) &= n \cdot W_1^n \frac{(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^n)^2}{(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^n)^2 + (W_2^n)^2} + z \cdot W_1^p \frac{(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^p)^2}{(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^p)^2 + (W_2^p)^2} \\[6pt] W_d(E) &= n \cdot D_1^n \frac{(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^n)^2 e^{-D_2^n (E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^n)}} {(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^n)^2 + (D_3^n)^2} + z \cdot D_1^p \frac{(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^p)^2 e^{-D_2^p (E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^p)}} {(E_{\text{eff}} - E_f^p)^2 + (D_3^p)^2} \end{aligned} \]

where \(n\) and \(z\) are the neutron and proton numbers of the fragment. After summing over neutrons and protons, the radius and diffuseness parameters are averaged over the fragment mass number \(a\):

\[ R_v = \frac{n R_v^n + z R_v^p}{a}, \qquad a_v = \frac{n a_v^n + z a_v^p}{a}, \qquad R_d = \frac{n R_d^n + z R_d^p}{a}, \qquad a_d = \frac{n a_d^n + z a_d^p}{a} \]

5.3 Spin-0 Approximation for the Central Potential

KoningDelarocheOpticalModel.cc:257-265

The function setIncidentEnergyAndFragment(E_kin, pdg) calls calculate_om_parameters with two_s = 0. This has two important consequences:

  • Spin-orbit eigenvative is zero: \( \langle \mathbf{l}\cdot\mathbf{s} \rangle = 0 \)
  • Only central potential terms contribute to Wv, Wd, Rv, av, Rd, ad
Physics justification: The volume-averaged imaginary potential computed in the exciton model is an angle-averaged absorption strength. The central potential already contains the dominant absorption physics; the spin-orbit contribution averages to zero over the Fermi sphere and is negligible for internal transition rates.

6. Volume-Averaged Imaginary Potential

transition_rates.cpp:47-68

The radial form of the imaginary optical model potential is (Eq. 13.29 of the TALYS manual):

\[ W(r) = W_v \cdot f_v(r) - 4 a_d W_d \cdot \frac{d}{dr}f_s(r) \]

where \(f_v(r)\) and \(f_s(r)\) are Woods–Saxon form factors:

\[ f_{v,s}(r) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\!\left(\frac{r - R_{v,s}}{a_{v,s}}\right)} \]

The volume-averaged imaginary potential is (Eq. 13.30):

\[ \langle W \rangle = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^\infty W(r)\, f_v(r)\, r^2\, dr} {\displaystyle\int_0^\infty f_v(r)\, r^2\, dr} \]

Following TALYS bonetti.f90, the integral is evaluated with a 50-bin midpoint rule at dr = 0.4 fm (transition_rates.cpp:50-67):

\[ \langle W \rangle \approx \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{50} W(r_i)\, f_v(r_i)\, r_i^2} {\sum_{i=1}^{50} f_v(r_i)\, r_i^2}, \qquad r_i = (i - \tfrac12)\,\Delta r \]

The derivative of the surface Woods–Saxon is computed analytically:

\[ \frac{d}{dr}f_s(r) = -\frac{e^{(r-R_d)/a_d}}{a_d\left(1 + e^{(r-R_d)/a_d}\right)^2} \]

7. Collision Rate in the Integrand

transition_rates.cpp:238-256 (pair creation) and transition_rates.cpp:486-506 (conversion)

The OMP branch in the integrand replaces the constant M² term with:

\[ \lambda_{\text{collision}} = \frac{2}{\hbar} \cdot \text{wompfac} \cdot \langle W \rangle \]

The full integrand for pair creation (Eq. 13.17) becomes:

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d\lambda_{\pi^+}}{de} &= \lambda_{\text{collision}} \cdot g_{\pi} \cdot \omega_r(U - e) \cdot R_{\text{hole}} \\[4pt] R_{\text{hole}} &= \begin{cases} 1 & \text{(particle-type sub-channel)} \\[2pt] \displaystyle \frac{\omega_{\text{hole}}(e)}{\omega_{\text{particle}}(e)} & \text{(hole-type sub-channel)} \end{cases} \end{aligned} \]

The hole-channel ratio \(\omega_{\text{hole}}/\omega_{\text{particle}}\) is retained in OMP mode because it reflects the probability that the created hole configuration is actually populated — a purely statistical factor independent of the collision mechanism (Eq. 13.33).

For conversion (Eq. 13.19), the analogous factor is \(\omega_{\text{created}}/\omega_{\text{ref}}\).

9. Build System

Makefile

The KD model source file is compiled as part of the lambda executable. The Makefile links against GSL (required by the KD model's Coulomb wavefunctions and transmission coefficients, even though those functions are not called in the wvol-only path) and ROOT (required by MARLEY's event I/O classes). The `-ffunction-sections -Wl,-dead_strip` flags (Linux/macOS respectively) would strip unused functions, but the vtable references all virtual functions, so the linker cannot remove them.

The KD model constructor uses a simplified mass target_mass_ = A · 931.494061 MeV (stored, not used in the wvol-only OMP kernel path). Separation energies for the integrand come from the full MARLEY MassTable singleton, which reads mass_table.js from $MARLEY/data/ at runtime. The make run target sets MARLEY=$(abspath ../marley-private) automatically.

10. Summary of Physics Approximations

Approximation Justification Potential impact
Spin-0 central potential only Angle-averaged absorption; spin-orbit averages to zero over Fermi sphere < 5% for medium-mass nuclei
MARLEY MassTable separation energies AME experimental masses + liquid-drop fallback via MARLEY library Experimental accuracy near stability; liquid-drop ∼1–2 MeV far from stability
50-bin midpoint radial integral Matches TALYS bonetti.f90 exactly Negligible (< 0.1%) for smooth Woods–Saxon profiles
Wompfac rescaling Preserves M² relative channel strengths while replacing absolute scale Calibrated to TALYS; valid for default COMP = 0.55
Single KD model per call Compound nucleus dominates — residual differences are small May affect cross-type channels near thresholds
